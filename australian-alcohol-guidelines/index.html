Australian alcohol guidelines: how much is actually 'safe'?

Ask ten Australians how much drinking is "fine" and you'll get ten different answers. The official answer fits in one sentence: no more than 10 standard drinks a week, and no more than 4 on any one day. Here's where those numbers come from, what they really mean, and why most of us are counting our drinks wrong.

The guidelines in one line

Australia's National Health and Medical Research Council (NHMRC) recommends that to reduce the risk of harm from alcohol-related disease or injury, healthy adults should drink:

  • No more than 10 standard drinks a week, and
  • No more than 4 standard drinks on any one day.

Both limits apply at the same time — it's an "and", not an "or". The guidelines also carry a line that's easy to skim past but is really the whole point: the less you drink, the lower your risk of harm from alcohol. Ten a week isn't a target or an allowance. It's a ceiling.

The other two guidelines are simpler: people under 18 should not drink alcohol at all, and women who are pregnant, planning a pregnancy or breastfeeding should not drink — not drinking is the safest option for their baby.

First, get your counting right

The guidelines only work if you know what a standard drink actually is — and this is where most people go wrong. An Australian standard drink contains 10 grams of pure alcohol, which is roughly what a healthy body processes in an hour. As the Australian Government's standard drinks guide shows, very few real-world serves equal exactly one:

ServeStandard drinks
Pot of full-strength beer (285ml, 4.8%)1.1
Stubby or can of full-strength beer (375ml, 4.8%)1.4
Can of mid-strength beer (375ml, 3.5%)1.0
Small glass of wine (100ml, 13%)1.0
Typical restaurant pour of wine (150ml, 13%)1.5
Nip of spirits (30ml, 40%)1.0
Pre-mixed can (375ml, 5%)1.5

The pattern is obvious: almost everything you're handed is more than one standard drink. Four stubbies feels like "four drinks" but is actually 5.6 — already over the daily limit. Two generous restaurant pours of wine can be three standard drinks. If you want to check any drink, the maths is: volume (litres) × alcohol % × 0.789. Or check the label — every bottle, can and cask sold in Australia must state its standard drinks.

Why there are two numbers, not one

The weekly and daily limits guard against different harms.

The weekly limit of 10 targets long-term disease risk — the slow accumulation of damage that shows up years later as cancer, liver disease, heart disease and stroke. This risk scales with your total consumption over time, which is why it's framed per week.

The daily limit of 4 targets injury on the night — falls, fights, road crashes, alcohol poisoning. Most alcohol-related injury happens during or straight after a session, and the risk climbs steeply with each drink past that point.

That's why you can't "bank" your drinks. Zero beers Monday to Friday doesn't make 10 schooners on Saturday safe — you'd stay under the weekly cap while more than doubling the daily one, and it's the big-night pattern that puts people in emergency departments.

Where the numbers come from

The current guidelines were released by the NHMRC in December 2020 after a review of the international evidence. The logic behind the specific numbers is worth knowing: if you follow both limits, your lifetime chance of dying from an alcohol-related condition stays below about 1 in 100. That's the risk threshold the NHMRC judged acceptable — not zero risk, but low.

Notice the framing. The guidelines don't claim 10 drinks a week is safe — they say it keeps your risk of harm low. That distinction matters more than it sounds.

Is any amount actually safe?

Strictly speaking, no. A landmark global analysis published in The Lancet concluded that no level of alcohol consumption improves health, and in 2023 the World Health Organization stated plainly that when it comes to health, there is no safe amount of alcohol — risk starts from the first drink. The old idea that a daily red wine protects your heart hasn't survived modern research methods.

That doesn't mean one beer will do you meaningful harm — at low levels the risk is small. It means the honest way to read the guidelines is as a risk dial, not an on/off switch: every drink under 10 a week turns the dial down further.

How Australia compares

Australia's guidelines sit mid-field internationally. The UK recommends no more than 14 units a week — a UK unit is smaller (8 grams of alcohol), so that works out to about 11 Australian standard drinks. Canada went much further in 2023, with national guidance describing 2 or fewer drinks a week as low risk. The direction of travel worldwide is consistent: as the evidence on alcohol and cancer has firmed up, recommended limits keep coming down, not up.

Staying under 10 without thinking about it

Most people who drift over the guidelines aren't big drinkers — they're normal drinkers who never count. A few habits close the gap, and healthdirect's safe-drinking tips echo the same themes:

  • Count standard drinks, not glasses. Log what you actually poured — a heavy hand with wine at home can make "two glasses" into four standard drinks.
  • Keep alcohol-free days. Two or three zero days a week makes the weekly cap almost take care of itself.
  • Downshift, don't abstain. Swapping full-strength for mid-strength beer cuts nearly a third of the alcohol without changing the ritual.
  • Alternate with water on nights out — it slows your pace and halves your count.
  • Track it. A running weekly tally is the difference between guessing and knowing. Most people who start logging drinks are surprised in the wrong direction.
The guidelines are not a driving guide. Staying under 4 standard drinks in a day can still put you over the 0.05 BAC limit, depending on your size, sex and timing. Never use the guidelines — or how you feel — to judge whether you're right to drive. If in doubt, don't.

Frequently asked questions

How many standard drinks a week is safe in Australia?

The NHMRC recommends no more than 10 standard drinks a week and no more than 4 on any one day to reduce the risk of harm. No level of drinking is completely risk-free — less is always lower risk.

Can I save all 10 drinks for one big night?

No. The two limits apply together. Ten drinks in one session breaches the 4-a-day limit and sharply raises your risk of injury, even though the weekly total looks compliant.

How much alcohol is in one standard drink?

Ten grams of pure alcohol — about a 285ml pot of full-strength beer, a 100ml glass of wine or a 30ml nip of spirits. Most real-world serves are more than one standard drink.

What are the guidelines for pregnancy and under-18s?

Zero in both cases. Under-18s should not drink, and women who are pregnant, planning a pregnancy or breastfeeding should not drink at all.

Does staying under 10 drinks a week mean drinking is healthy?

No. The guidelines keep your lifetime risk of alcohol-related death below about 1 in 100 — they're a risk ceiling, not a health endorsement. Research supports that no level of alcohol improves health.
