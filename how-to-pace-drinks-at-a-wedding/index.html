How to pace your drinks at a wedding (without missing the fun)

A wedding is the longest drinking day most of us ever have: champagne at three, wine through dinner, an open bar until midnight. Nobody plans to overdo it — the day is just built that way. Here's how to pace yourself from the ceremony to the last dance, and still be dancing at the end.

Why weddings catch people out

On a normal night out you decide when to buy each drink. At a wedding, drinks come to you — trays of sparkling at the canapés, wine poured with dinner, glasses quietly topped up before they're empty. That removes the two natural brakes on your drinking: the walk to the bar and the sight of an empty glass.

Add the length of the day and the maths gets serious. A 3pm ceremony with a midnight finish is nine hours. Even a modest-sounding pace of one drink an hour would be nine standard drinks — more than double the four-a-day maximum in Australia's NHMRC alcohol guidelines, and nearly a whole week's worth in one day. And that's the restrained version.

Three more traps are specific to weddings:

  • The empty-stomach start. Canapés are not a meal. Sparkling wine on a near-empty stomach hits your bloodstream fast, so the first two drinks of the day do more work than any two later on.
  • The top-up trap. When your glass never empties, you lose count. Ask anyone the morning after a wedding how many drinks they had — the honest answer is usually "no idea".
  • Toasts and rounds. Speeches, toasts and "one more with the groomsmen" add drinks you never chose. They're social defaults, not decisions.

The champagne maths

Pacing starts with knowing what you're actually holding. An Australian standard drink is 10 grams of pure alcohol — roughly what your body processes in an hour — and, as the Australian Government's standard drinks guide shows, almost nothing served at a wedding equals exactly one:

Glass of sparkling (150ml, 12%)1.4
Wine with dinner (150ml, 13%)1.5
Stubby of full-strength beer (375ml, 4.8%)1.4
Gin and tonic (30ml nip, 40%)1.0
Espresso martini (typical recipe)~1.6
Can of mid-strength beer (375ml, 3.5%)1.0

So "a glass at the canapés, a couple with dinner and a few at the bar" — six drinks by feel — is realistically eight to nine standard drinks. If you're wondering what's in the cocktail list, our cocktail standard-drinks guide breaks down the common ones.

A pacing plan that actually works

Before the ceremony

  • Eat a proper meal. Food in your stomach slows how quickly alcohol is absorbed, which flattens the early spike from that first champagne. Don't bank on canapés.
  • Pick your number. Decide your total for the whole day before the first tray comes around, and make it realistic. A limit chosen at 1pm survives; one improvised at 9pm doesn't.
  • Sort your ride home now. Deciding how you'll get home before you start drinking is the single most important call of the day (more below).

During the reception

  • One an hour, tops. Your body clears roughly one standard drink an hour, so this ceiling keeps you close to level rather than climbing all night. Remember most serves are about 1.4–1.5, so "one an hour" really means sipping it slowly across the hour.
  • Alternate with water — every time. One water between every alcoholic drink halves your count over the day and helps with the hangover. healthdirect's safe-drinking tips put this near the top of the list for good reason.
  • Beat the top-up. Keep your glass at least half full when you're pacing (staff refill the emptiest glasses first), or put your hand over it with a smile. Nobody argues with a hand.
  • Sip the toasts, don't skol them. Raising your glass is the toast. What's in it — or how much of it you drink — is nobody's business.
  • Skip the shots. A round of shots is one to two standard drinks in ten seconds with no brakes. It's also how good nights end early.
  • Downshift late. After dinner, switch to mid-strength beer, a spritz, or a soft drink in a nice glass. By 10pm nobody knows or cares what you're drinking — they care whether you're on the dance floor.
In the wedding party? Your day starts earlier — sometimes with mimosas at breakfast — so the maths is harsher. Count everything before the ceremony as part of your day's total, stay light until the photos and speeches are done, and remember you're in every photo. Future-you will be grateful.

Getting home — and the morning after

Plan not to drive. Full stop. Even disciplined pacing across a nine-hour day can leave your BAC above 0.05, and no app, chart or rule of thumb can tell you you're safe to drive — including ours. Book a room, share a lift with a sober driver, or line up a rideshare before the reception ends and everyone wants one at once.

The trap people miss is the next morning. Alcohol clears at a fixed rate of about one standard drink an hour, and nothing — coffee, breakfast, a swim — speeds it up. Finish a big night at 1am and you can still be over the limit at 9am. Before you drive to the recovery brunch, check our guide to how long it takes to get back to zero.

Estimates are estimates. BAC calculators — including Drink Buddy's — give a guide based on averages, not a measurement. Your actual BAC depends on your body, food, medication and more. If there's any doubt at all, don't drive.

Enjoying the wedding is the point

None of this is about drinking less for its own sake. It's about being present for the speeches, remembering the first dance, and being the guest who made the night better rather than the story people tell at the next wedding. The couple spent a year planning this day — the best gift you can give them on the night is turning up to all of it.

If you want the running count handled for you, log each drink as it lands in your hand. Seeing "6.5 standard drinks" on your screen at 9pm is the nudge that no amount of good intentions can match — and how you stay under the Australian guidelines the other 51 weekends of the year, too.

Frequently asked questions

How many standard drinks is a glass of champagne at a wedding?

A typical 150ml glass of sparkling at 12% is about 1.4 standard drinks. With top-ups, a "couple of glasses" during canapés is often three to four standard drinks.

How do I stop waiters topping up my glass?

Keep your glass more than half full, briefly cover it with your hand, or ask for water or soda in a wine glass. A glass in your hand stops most offers — nobody checks what's in it.

Do I have to drink alcohol for the toasts?

No. Raising your glass is the toast; sipping — or toasting with water — is perfectly polite at any Australian wedding.

Can I drive home from a wedding if I pace my drinks?

Plan not to drive at all. Even careful pacing can leave you over 0.05, and estimates are only estimates. Sort accommodation or a lift before the day starts, and be wary of the morning after — you can still be over the limit at breakfast.

What should I do if I'm in the wedding party?

Eat properly, count everything from breakfast onwards as part of your total, stay light until the formalities are done, and stick to about one standard drink an hour with water in between.
