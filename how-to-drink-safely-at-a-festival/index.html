How to drink safely at a music festival

A festival day is a marathon, not a sprint: 8 to 12 hours on your feet, often in the sun, often dancing, with a bar never more than fifty metres away. Drink at your normal Friday-night pace for that long and the maths gets ugly fast. Here's how to make it to the headliner — and home again.

Why alcohol hits harder at a festival

Three things gang up on you at a festival that don't on an ordinary night out.

The heat. Alcohol is a diuretic — every drink pulls more fluid out of you than it puts in. Add sun, crowds and dancing, and you're losing water from both ends of the equation: sweating it out while alcohol tells your kidneys to get rid of more. healthdirect lists the warning signs of dehydration as headache, thirst, dizziness, urinating less than usual and dark-coloured urine — all of which are easy to mistake for "just having a big day".

The hours. Gates might open at midday and the headliner finishes at 11pm. A modest-sounding one drink per hour over that stretch is 10 or more standard drinks — more than the Australian guidelines recommend in an entire week, and well past the point where judgement, balance and heat tolerance are all impaired.

The lost count. Festival drinks come in cans, plastic cups and someone-passed-it-to-you formats, in varying strengths, spread over many hours. Almost nobody keeps an accurate tally in their head. And you can't pace what you're not counting.

Heat plus alcohol is its own risk. Alcohol interferes with the way your body regulates temperature, so drinking makes you worse at handling a hot day at exactly the moment you need to be better at it. Queensland Health's festival survival guide puts shade breaks, water and sun protection at the top of the list for good reason.

Know what's actually in your can

In Australia a standard drink is 10 grams of pure alcohol, and every label must state how many standard drinks the container holds. At a festival bar you rarely get to study the label — so learn the common numbers before you go:

Two takeaways from that table. First, mid-strength is the festival cheat code: it's the only common option where one can genuinely equals one standard drink, which makes counting trivial. Second, premix cans are the trap — two strong ones equal nearly five standard drinks, which is a big night's worth before the sun's even down.

A pacing plan that survives contact with the day

Plans like "I'll take it easy" dissolve by the second act. Concrete rules hold up better:

  • Eat before your first drink — a proper meal, not a snack. Food slows alcohol absorption; drinking on an empty stomach at midday is how people peak before the main stage opens.
  • One water for every alcoholic drink. The Alcohol and Drug Foundation recommends alternating alcohol with non-alcoholic drinks at events — festivals have free water refill stations, so bring an empty bottle and use them.
  • Set a number before you arrive — a total for the whole day, not a vibe. Decide it sober at breakfast, not thirsty at the bar.
  • Anchor drinks to sets, not rounds. One drink per act you actually watch is a pace; keeping up with the fastest drinker in your group is not.
  • Go mid-strength in the afternoon and save full-strength for the evening when the sun's down — same ritual, roughly two-thirds the alcohol.
  • Log every drink as you buy it. Ten seconds at the bar beats trying to reconstruct your day at 9pm.

Look after your drink — and your mates

Crowded venues are exactly where drink spiking happens. Keep your drink in your hand and in your sight, don't accept open drinks from strangers, and if you've lost track of your cup for even a few minutes, tip it out and buy another — it's cheaper than the alternative. The same goes for your group: watch each other's drinks, agree on a meeting point in case phones die or the crowd separates you, and check in on anyone who suddenly seems far drunker than what they've had should explain.

If someone does become confused, drowsy or unresponsive, get them to the medical tent. Festival medics deal with this all day, they're free, and no one gets in trouble for seeking help.

Getting home is part of the plan

If you've been drinking across a festival day, you are not driving home — that decision was made hours ago, whether you've acknowledged it or not. Your body clears roughly one standard drink per hour and nothing speeds that up, so a big day can also leave you over the limit the next morning. Sort transport before you arrive: a designated driver who's actually not drinking, the festival shuttle, public transport, or a pre-booked ride with a charged phone to find it.

Camping festivals deserve special mention: driving out at 8am after a huge Saturday is one of the classic morning-after traps. If you're packing up early, do the maths on last night's drinks first — our morning-after driving guide covers exactly this.

Multi-day festivals: back-to-back big days compound. Dehydration carries over, sleep in a tent is poor, and day two starts with yesterday's alcohol possibly still on board. Make day one the biggest, front-load your water, and treat the final morning's drive as its own drinking decision.

Frequently asked questions

Why does alcohol hit harder at a festival?

Heat and dancing dehydrate you while alcohol is already acting as a diuretic, and festival days run 8–12 hours — so a normal drinking pace adds up to far more standard drinks than a normal night out, in conditions your body handles worse.

How much water should I drink at a festival?

At least one cup of water per alcoholic drink, plus more when it's hot or you're dancing. Use the free refill stations, and treat headache, dizziness or dark urine as a signal to stop, hydrate and find shade.

How many standard drinks are in festival cans and cups?

Roughly 1.4 for a full-strength beer, 1.0 for a mid-strength, 1.3 for a cider, 1.5 for a festival wine pour, and anywhere from 1.2 to 2.4 for premix spirit cans. The label always states the exact number.

Can I drive home after a festival?

Not if you've been drinking through the day — and depending on how much, possibly not the next morning either. Your body clears about one standard drink per hour. Arrange transport before you arrive.

How do I keep my drink safe at a festival?

Keep it in your hand and in sight, never accept open drinks from strangers, and replace any drink you've lost track of. Look out for your mates' drinks too.
