Calories in alcohol: beer, wine and spirits compared

Most people watching their weight count what's on the plate and forget what's in the glass. But alcohol carries 29 kilojoules (7 calories) per gram — nearly as much as pure fat — and unlike food, it doesn't fill you up. Here's what your usual drinks really cost, and how to cut back without giving them up.

Why alcohol is so energy-dense

Gram for gram, alcohol is the second most energy-dense thing you can consume. Fat tops the list at 37 kJ per gram; alcohol comes next at 29 kJ (7 calories) per gram — well ahead of carbohydrates and protein at about 17 kJ (4 calories) per gram. That's why the UK's NHS points out that a standard glass of wine can rival a slice of cake for energy content.

In Australia, one standard drink contains 10 grams of pure alcohol — so every standard drink delivers roughly 290 kJ (about 69 calories) from the alcohol alone, before you count any sugar in the drink or the mixer. Health promotion bodies like LiveLighter call these "empty kilojoules": plenty of energy, no useful nutrients.

Quick maths: one Australian standard drink = 10g of alcohol ≈ 290 kJ (69 cal) from the alcohol itself. A drink with 1.6 standard drinks in it — like a schooner of full-strength beer — starts at ~460 kJ before the carbohydrates in the beer are added.

Two patterns jump out. First, size and strength matter more than drink type — a big glass of strong beer beats a nip of spirits several times over. Second, sugar stacks on top of alcohol: premixes and creamy cocktails are the most expensive drinks in the fridge because you're paying for both.

How it adds up over a year

Single drinks sound harmless; habits are where the kilojoules hide. Two schooners on Friday night and two more on Saturday is about 2,460 kJ a weekend — roughly 128,000 kJ over a year. With the average adult needing about 8,700 kJ a day, according to healthdirect, that's the energy equivalent of around two extra weeks of food, every year, from four beers a weekend.

The NHS runs the same maths for wine: four bottles of 12% wine a month adds up to around 32,400 calories (roughly 136,000 kJ) a year. None of it registers as "eating", which is exactly why liquid kilojoules are so easy to overlook.

It's not just the kilojoules in the glass

Alcohol works against your waistline in a few indirect ways too:

  • It stimulates appetite. A few drinks make the late-night kebab or extra chips far more likely — the food eaten around drinking often outweighs the drink itself.
  • Your liver burns alcohol first. Because alcohol is mildly toxic, your body prioritises clearing it — pausing normal fat metabolism while it does. Energy from food eaten alongside is more readily stored as fat.
  • It lowers resolve. Healthy intentions rarely survive the third round. Decisions about food, another drink and tomorrow's exercise all get looser as BAC rises.

Cutting liquid kilojoules without quitting

You don't have to go dry to make a real dent. The levers are the same ones that reduce alcohol harm generally:

  • Count your standard drinks. Every standard drink is ~290 kJ from alcohol alone, so your drink count is also a rough kilojoule count. Tracking beats guessing.
  • Downsize the serve. A pot instead of a schooner, a 100ml pour instead of a generous 180ml one — smaller serves cut kilojoules and standard drinks in one move.
  • Drop the strength. Mid-strength beer carries roughly a third less alcohol — and correspondingly less energy — than full-strength.
  • Fix the mixer. Soda water with fresh lime instead of cola or tonic removes the sugar half of a spirit drink's kilojoules.
  • Alternate with water. A glass of water between drinks halves your drinking pace, which halves everything downstream.
  • Keep alcohol-free days. Australia's NHMRC guidelines recommend no more than 10 standard drinks a week and no more than 4 on any one day — staying inside those limits caps your liquid kilojoules at a manageable level too.
A note on "diet" drinking: skipping meals to "save" kilojoules for drinks is a bad trade. Drinking on an empty stomach pushes your BAC higher and faster, and the appetite rebound usually claws the kilojoules back later in the night — with interest.

Frequently asked questions

How many calories are in a beer?

A schooner (425ml) of full-strength beer is roughly 615 kJ (147 calories); a pot (285ml) about 410 kJ (98 calories). Stronger beers and bigger serves carry proportionally more — a UK pint of 5% beer can reach 930 kJ (222 calories).

Does alcohol make you gain weight?

It can. Alcohol is nearly as energy-dense as fat, provides no useful nutrients, stimulates appetite, and pauses your body's fat-burning while the liver clears it. Regular drinking makes storing excess energy as fat considerably easier.

Which alcoholic drink is lowest in calories?

Per serve, a 30ml nip of straight spirits (~275 kJ / 65 calories) — if you skip sugary mixers. Light beer and small serves of dry wine are also lower-kilojoule choices. Premixes and creamy cocktails sit at the top of the range.

Do zero-carb spirits have zero calories?

No. "Zero carbs" doesn't mean zero energy — the alcohol itself is the main source. A 30ml nip of 40% spirits carries about 9.5g of alcohol, roughly 275 kJ, before any mixer.

How can I cut alcohol calories without quitting drinking?

Reduce total standard drinks: smaller serves, lower-strength options, soda water instead of sugary mixers, water between drinks, and an accurate count as you go rather than a generous estimate afterwards.
