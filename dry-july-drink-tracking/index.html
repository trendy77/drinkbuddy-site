How to track your drinks during Dry July (and make it stick)

An alcohol-free July is a great reset. But the people who get lasting change out of it do one thing differently: they track — before, during and after the month. Here's how.

What Dry July actually is

Dry July is an Australian fundraising campaign: participants give up alcohol for the month of July and raise money to support people affected by cancer. Plenty of people also do an unofficial dry month for the health benefits alone — better sleep, more energy, a clearer head and a lighter wallet all show up within a couple of weeks for most regular drinkers.

Either way, the challenge is the same: 31 days, zero drinks. And the biggest predictor of whether the month changes anything long-term is whether you can see what changed.

Step 1: Know your baseline (even if July's already started)

You can't measure a reset without a "before". If you tracked your drinks in June, you already know your typical week — say, 14 standard drinks. That number is what makes the end of July satisfying: it's the difference between "I didn't drink for a month" and "I usually have 60 drinks a month, and this month I had zero".

Already mid-July with no baseline? No problem — reconstruct a typical week from memory using a standard drinks calculator, jot it down, and you'll still have a comparison point for August.

Step 2: Track the zeros

It sounds odd to open a drink tracker to log nothing, but the streak is the point. A visible run of alcohol-free days is one of the most reliable motivators in habit change — the longer the chain, the less you want to break it. Check your weekly chart each Sunday and watch the flat line grow.

Step 3: Handle Golden Tickets and slip-ups honestly

The official Dry July campaign offers a Golden Ticket — a donation that buys you one night off without breaking your challenge. If you use one (a wedding, a big birthday), log those drinks like any other night. The data is for you, not for judgement, and an honest log keeps the rest of the month meaningful.

Slipped without a ticket? Log it and move on. One drink on day 19 doesn't erase 18 alcohol-free days — but quietly abandoning the tracker because of one bad night usually erases the rest of the month. The log is the difference between a slip and a collapse.

Step 4: Notice what the month is telling you

Somewhere around week two or three, most people notice patterns: the Friday 5pm itch, the "I can't watch footy without a beer" reflex, the social pressure at dinner. Write these down or note them in your tracker. They're not failures — they're a map of your triggers, and they're the most valuable thing Dry July produces.

Step 5: Don't let August undo it

The classic Dry July failure mode is the August 1st blowout, followed by a quiet return to old habits by September. The fix is to decide your August rules before July ends:

  • Set a weekly limit. The Australian guideline is no more than 10 standard drinks a week, and no more than 4 on any one day. After a dry month, many people comfortably land well under that.
  • Keep logging. The habit is already built — 31 days of it. People who keep tracking after a dry month hold onto most of their reduction; people who stop tend to drift back within weeks.
  • Keep two or three alcohol-free days a week. You've just proven you can do 31 in a row. Two a week is easy mode.

Frequently asked questions

What is Dry July?

An Australian fundraiser where participants go alcohol-free for July to support people affected by cancer — though many people do a dry month independently for the health reset.

Why track drinks during a month where I'm not drinking?

The streak keeps you motivated during the month, and the baseline-versus-July comparison is what makes the change stick afterwards.

What if I slip up?

Log it honestly and continue. One drink is a data point, not a verdict. Golden Tickets exist in the official campaign for planned exceptions.

How do I keep the benefits going after July?

Set a weekly limit before August starts, keep logging, and keep a few alcohol-free days each week.
