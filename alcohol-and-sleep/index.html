Alcohol and sleep: why a nightcap wrecks your rest

A drink before bed feels like it helps you drift off — and it does, a little. But alcohol quietly sabotages the back half of your night, suppressing the deep, restorative sleep your brain and body actually need. Here's what the science says.

The nightcap paradox

Alcohol is a sedative, so a couple of drinks genuinely do make you feel drowsy and can shorten the time it takes to fall asleep. That's the appeal of the nightcap, and it's why so many people reach for one after a stressful day. The catch is that falling asleep faster is only a small part of a good night's rest — and it comes at the cost of everything that happens next.

Once you're asleep, alcohol reshapes the entire structure of your night. Researchers call this "sleep architecture": the ordered cycle through light sleep, deep slow-wave sleep and REM sleep that your brain repeats several times before morning. Alcohol front-loads the sedation, then leaves you paying it back with interest in the early hours.

What alcohol does to your sleep stages

In the first half of the night, alcohol pushes you into more deep, slow-wave sleep while suppressing REM sleep — the dreaming stage most closely tied to memory consolidation and emotional processing. A 2024 systematic review and meta-analysis of alcohol's effects on sleep found that drinking reduced REM sleep by an average of roughly 11 minutes, and the more you drank, the larger the reduction became.

Then, as your liver clears the alcohol from your bloodstream, the sedative effect fades and your sleep rebounds in the opposite direction. The second half of the night becomes lighter and more broken, with a surge of REM sleep and noticeably more time spent awake. This is the classic "wide awake at 3am" experience — often accompanied by a racing heart, a full bladder or a faint sense of anxiety.

The rebound effect in one line: alcohol borrows deep sleep from the start of your night and repays it with fragmented, restless sleep before dawn. You spend the same hours in bed, but wake up less rested.

How much is too much for sleep?

Sleep disruption isn't reserved for heavy sessions. Studies have observed measurable reductions in REM sleep starting at around two standard drinks, with the effect growing as consumption climbs. A "standard drink" in Australia contains 10 grams of pure alcohol, whether it's in beer, wine or spirits — a fact worth remembering, because a generously poured glass of wine can easily be closer to two.

For context, Australia's National Health and Medical Research Council guidelines recommend healthy adults drink no more than 10 standard drinks a week and no more than 4 on any single day to reduce the risk of harm. Those limits are set for long-term health rather than sleep specifically, but sleep is one of the first things to improve when people drink less. The Alcohol and Drug Foundation notes that drinking well below these levels — or not at all — reliably supports better rest.

The knock-on effects

Poor sleep architecture doesn't just leave you tired. Because alcohol relaxes the muscles in your throat and airway, it can worsen snoring and make sleep apnoea more severe — one reason a big night often ends with a partner nudging you awake. Reduced REM sleep is linked to poorer memory, mood and concentration the next day, which can compound the general sluggishness of a hangover.

There's also a vicious cycle to watch for. People who drink to fall asleep can find their sleep worsens over time, tempting them to drink more to compensate. Sleep experts at the Sleep Foundation report that the overwhelming majority of regular evening drinkers experience at least one sleep-related problem.

A note on the morning after. If you've had a big night, remember that your blood alcohol content can still be above the legal limit when you wake up — sleep does not speed up how fast your body clears alcohol. Feeling rested is not the same as being sober enough to drive.

How to protect your sleep

You don't have to give up drinking entirely to sleep better, but a few habits make a real difference:

  • Leave a gap before bed. Sleep researchers generally suggest finishing your last drink at least three to four hours before you sleep, so your body can metabolise most of it first.
  • Space your drinks and hydrate. Alternating alcoholic drinks with water slows your intake and softens the middle-of-the-night dehydration that wakes you up.
  • Keep some nights alcohol-free. Even a few dry nights a week let your sleep architecture reset — many people notice deeper, more refreshing sleep within days.
  • Track what you actually drink. It's easy to underestimate. Knowing you've had four standard drinks rather than "a couple of wines" helps you connect the dots between last night and this morning.

Frequently asked questions

Does alcohol help you sleep?

It helps you fall asleep faster, but it worsens sleep quality. The sedative effect fades partway through the night and leaves you with lighter, more broken sleep and less REM. On balance, it costs you more rest than it gives.

How does alcohol affect REM sleep?

Alcohol suppresses REM in the first half of the night — by around 11 minutes on average, and more at higher doses. REM is the stage tied to memory and emotional processing, so losing it contributes to next-day grogginess and poor focus.

How long before bed should I stop drinking?

Aim to finish drinking three to four hours before bed so most of the alcohol has cleared. Even then, a heavy session will still affect how restful your sleep is.

Why do I wake up at 3am after drinking?

As your body clears the alcohol, its sedative effect reverses into a rebound — a surge of REM sleep and more wakefulness in the second half of the night. That's why a few drinks so often means waking in the small hours.
