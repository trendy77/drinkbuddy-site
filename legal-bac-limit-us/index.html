What's the legal BAC limit for driving in the US?

In every US state it's illegal to drive with a blood alcohol concentration of 0.08% or higher — except Utah, where the limit is 0.05%. But the full picture has more layers: stricter limits for commercial and under-21 drivers, and the fact that you can be arrested well below the number.

The limits at a glance

DriverPer se BAC limitNotes
Adult drivers (21+)0.08 g/dLAll 50 states, DC and Puerto Rico — except Utah
Adult drivers in Utah0.05 g/dLThe strictest state limit, in force since December 2018
Commercial drivers (CDL)0.04 g/dLFederal rule — applies nationwide when driving a commercial vehicle
Drivers under 210.00–0.02 g/dL"Zero tolerance" laws in every state; the exact threshold varies

"Per se" means the number alone is enough: test at or above the limit and you're legally intoxicated, regardless of how well you think you were driving. Many states also add enhanced penalties at high BACs (commonly 0.15 and above) — larger fines, longer license suspensions and mandatory ignition interlock devices.

Why Utah is different

Utah became the first — and so far only — state to lower its limit from 0.08 to 0.05, effective December 30, 2018. The results drew national attention: a National Highway Traffic Safety Administration (NHTSA) study found that in the first year under the new law, total crashes in Utah fell 9.6%, injury crashes fell 10.8% and fatal crashes fell 19.8%.

Other states have noticed. In early 2026, Washington's Senate passed a bill to lower its limit to 0.05, though it stalled in the House. Illinois, New York, Hawaii and Delaware have all seen similar proposals. None have passed yet — but if you're reading this a year or two from now, check your state's current law rather than assuming 0.08 still applies.

What 0.08 actually feels like

The uncomfortable truth about 0.08 is that most people don't feel dangerously drunk at that level — they feel loose, confident and "fine to drive." According to NHTSA, at 0.08 your muscle coordination, judgment, self-control, reasoning and short-term memory are all measurably impaired, and drivers at that level are about 4 times more likely to crash than sober drivers. At 0.15, crash risk is at least 12 times higher.

Impairment starts far earlier than the legal limit:

  • 0.02 — visual tracking and divided attention (doing two things at once) already decline.
  • 0.05 — reduced coordination, difficulty steering, slower response to emergencies.
  • 0.08 — poor muscle coordination, impaired judgment and memory, slower information processing.
  • 0.10+ — clear deterioration in reaction time, lane keeping and braking.
The toll is not abstract. 11,904 people died in alcohol-impaired driving crashes in the US in 2024 — about one person every 44 minutes. Roughly 30% of all US traffic deaths involve a drunk driver.

You can be under 0.08 and still get a DUI

The per se limit is a ceiling, not a free pass. Every state also allows impaired-driving charges below 0.08 when an officer can show that alcohol affected your driving — weaving, delayed reactions, failed field sobriety tests. And the risk below the limit is real: in 2024, 2,028 people were killed in US crashes where a driver had a BAC between just 0.01 and 0.07.

The CDC puts it plainly: alcohol can impair driving at amounts that don't even produce a noticeable buzz.

How many drinks is 0.08?

There's no universal answer, which is exactly why drink-counting is a poor safety strategy. Your BAC depends on your body weight, sex, how quickly you drank and whether you've eaten. As a reference point, a US standard drink contains about 14 grams (0.6 fl oz) of pure alcohol:

  • a 12 oz can of regular beer (~5% alcohol)
  • a 5 oz glass of wine (~12%)
  • a 1.5 oz shot of 80-proof spirits (40%)

A lighter person drinking on an empty stomach can pass 0.08 on three drinks in a couple of hours; a heavier person might test lower on the same intake. Craft beers, generous wine pours and cocktails routinely pack two standard drinks or more into one glass — so even careful counters usually underestimate.

Note for travelers: the US standard drink (14 g) is bigger than Australia's (10 g) and the UK's unit (8 g). If you're used to counting drinks at home, your mental math doesn't transfer.

A note on driving: no calculator, app or rule of thumb can tell you you're safe to drive. Impairment starts around 0.02 — well below every legal limit — and BAC estimates carry real uncertainty. If you've been drinking, don't drive. Arrange a designated driver, taxi or rideshare instead.

Frequently asked questions

What is the legal BAC limit for driving in the US?

0.08 g/dL in every state except Utah (0.05). Commercial drivers are limited to 0.04 nationwide, and under-21 drivers face zero-tolerance limits of 0.00–0.02.

Which state has the lowest BAC limit?

Utah, at 0.05 since December 2018. Fatal crashes there fell 19.8% in the first year, and several other states have since considered matching it.

How many drinks does it take to reach 0.08?

It varies too much to give a safe number — weight, sex, speed of drinking and food all matter. If you're driving, the only reliable number is zero.

Can I get a DUI below 0.08?

Yes. Every state allows impairment-based charges under the per se limit, and thousands die each year in crashes involving drivers below 0.08.

What counts as a standard drink in the US?

About 14 grams of pure alcohol — a 12 oz regular beer, 5 oz of wine, or a 1.5 oz shot of spirits.
