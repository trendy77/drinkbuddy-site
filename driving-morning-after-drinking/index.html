Can I drive the morning after drinking?

Maybe not. Your liver clears roughly one standard drink per hour, and nothing — not sleep, coffee, a cold shower or a big breakfast — speeds it up. After a heavy night, you can still be over the 0.05 limit well into the next morning, even if you feel completely fine.

Why "I slept it off" doesn't work

The most persistent morning-after myth is that a night's sleep resets you. It doesn't. Sleep changes how you feel, not how fast your body processes alcohol. Your liver works at a near-constant rate — a little under one standard drink per hour, according to the Alcohol and Drug Foundation — whether you're awake, asleep, exercising or lying very still feeling sorry for yourself.

Worse, your blood alcohol concentration (BAC) can keep rising for up to three hours after your last drink, as alcohol still in your stomach is absorbed. Stop drinking at midnight and your peak might not arrive until you're already in bed — which means the countdown to zero starts later than you think.

Feeling fine is not the same as being under 0.05. BAC measures the alcohol still in your blood, not how alert you feel. Plenty of people blow over the limit the morning after while feeling perfectly normal — and plenty more drive impaired while feeling "fine".

The morning-after maths

Here's the rough arithmetic, using the one-standard-drink-per-hour rule of thumb. In Australia a standard drink is 10 grams of pure alcohol — a 285 ml pot of full-strength beer, a 100 ml glass of wine, or a 30 ml nip of spirits. Real-world serves are usually bigger: a pint of full-strength beer is about 2.1 standard drinks, and a generous wine pour easily 1.5.

Two things about that table. First, those are earliest estimates — individual clearance rates vary with body size, sex, liver health, medication and more, so your zero hour could easily be later. Second, look at the bottom rows: a big Saturday night can leave you over the limit for the school run, the Sunday sport drop-off or the Monday commute. The National Road Safety Partnership Program notes it can take up to 18 hours for BAC to return to zero after a heavy session.

Yes, you can get breath tested at 8am

Random breath testing doesn't clock off at sunrise. Police run morning RBT operations on commuter routes, near worksites and after big event weekends precisely because morning-after drink driving is common. Transport for NSW's getting back to zero guidance is blunt about it: sobering up takes time, and it may simply not be safe — or legal — for you to drive the next day.

The penalties are the same at 8am as at 11pm: licence loss, fines, and for mid- and high-range offences, possible interlock orders or worse. For learner and provisional drivers the limit is zero — one leftover drink in your system is enough for an offence.

What actually helps (and what doesn't)

Doesn't work

  • Coffee — caffeine makes you feel more alert; your BAC doesn't move.
  • Cold showers and fresh air — you'll be a cleaner, colder person with the same BAC.
  • A big greasy breakfast — food slows absorption while you're drinking; it does nothing for alcohol already in your blood.
  • Exercise or sweating it out — only a tiny fraction of alcohol leaves via sweat. The liver does the rest, on its own schedule.
  • Vomiting — only removes alcohol not yet absorbed; it doesn't lower your BAC.

Does work

  • Time — the only thing that lowers BAC, as healthdirect confirms.
  • Counting your drinks — you can't do the morning-after maths if you don't know the numbers. Log as you go, note when you stop.
  • Finishing earlier — every hour you stop before midnight is an hour earlier you're back to zero.
  • Planning the morning the night before — if the maths says you won't be clear, arrange a lift, book a later start, or take the bus.

A realistic Saturday-night example

Say you're at a mate's barbecue. You have two pints of full-strength beer between 6pm and 8pm (about 4.2 standard drinks), share a bottle of wine over dinner (roughly 3.8 more), and finish with a nip of whisky at 11.30pm — call it 9 standard drinks, finishing at 11.30pm.

Your BAC likely peaks somewhere after midnight. From there, at a bit under one standard drink per hour, you're looking at roughly 9am or later before you're back to zero — and you'd have been over 0.05 for a good chunk of the early morning. If you'd planned a 7am start to drive the kids to sport, that plan needs to change: someone else drives, or sport starts without you.

A note on estimates: every figure on this page is a rule of thumb, not a guarantee. No calculator, app or table can tell you you're legal — let alone safe — to drive. If there's any doubt about the morning, there's no doubt: don't drive.

Frequently asked questions

Can I still be over the limit the morning after drinking?

Yes. The liver clears roughly one standard drink per hour and nothing accelerates it. After a heavy night it can take up to 18 hours from your last drink to reach zero, so being over 0.05 at breakfast is entirely possible.

Does sleeping sober you up?

No. Your liver works at the same rate asleep or awake. Sleep helps you feel better; it doesn't lower your BAC any faster.

How long does it take alcohol to leave your system?

Roughly one hour per standard drink after your last drink, with your BAC possibly still rising for up to three hours after you stop. Individual rates vary, so treat any estimate as a minimum.

Can police breath test me in the morning?

Yes. RBT operates around the clock, and morning operations are routine — especially on Sunday and Monday mornings after big weekends.

How can I work out when I'll be safe to drive?

Count your standard drinks, note when you stopped, allow at least an hour per drink, then add a margin. If the answer is close to when you need to leave, it's too close — make another plan.
